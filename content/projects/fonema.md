## Overview

**Fonema** is an **audio installation** about **artificial intelligence and human communication**. Recorded **voices** are sent through an **artificial neural network**; what returns is still recognisable as speech in grain and rhythm, but **semantic coherence dissolves** — enough remains to suggest a person, not enough to stabilise a sentence. The listening situation becomes **disorienting and slightly surreal**: you are always half a word away from comprehension.

The piece is not a demo of “good” speech AI. It uses the same family of tools that power classification and recognition systems, but bends them toward **loss of sense** rather than extraction of intent.

## How it works

Development involved a close pass over **neural architectures** and **audio signal processing** chains: how latent spaces and decision boundaries behave when you stop asking the model to label cleanly and instead ask it to **stay in the uncanny valley between word and noise**. The distorted voices can feel like overhearing a conversation when you are not tracking who is speaking — attention drifts, meaning thins, and technology sits in the middle of that gap.

In the room, the work is meant to **re-open questions** about how machines participate in how we **hear**, **trust**, and **interpret** spoken language, without resolving them into a moral slogan.

## Context

**Fonema** was realised as a **thesis project**: the research, prototyping, and installation form grew out of that academic frame rather than a commission or festival brief alone.

## Skills involved

The project brought together **model experimentation**, **real-time or installation-grade audio**, and **spatial listening** — deciding how long a listener can stay inside distortion before the piece becomes a gimmick, and how much technical transparency to keep in the presentation so the political reading stays attached to the pipeline, not only to the atmosphere.
