## Overview

**Wikyslavery** is a **net art** installation that intervenes on **Wikipedia** as both infrastructure and cultural symbol. A set of **real-time computational processes** pushes **misleading or inconsistent information** into **unspecified entries**, not to “win” an argument on a single page, but to keep a **moving field of transformed text** alive long enough that the social mechanisms around Wikipedia become visible: patrol workflows, revert speed, and the unpaid attention that holds the encyclopaedia together.

The provocation is directed at **volunteer labour** as much as at readers: the work occupies time that would otherwise go to ordinary maintenance, and makes the cost of care explicit. Underneath the stunt is a question about **trust**: what counts as a stable source when content can be automated, and who pays the price when it is not.

## How it works

Technically, the piece is built around **algorithms that run continuously**, selecting targets and mutating text in ways that remain **internally coherent enough to pass quick scans** while still triggering downstream correction. The installation frames Wikipedia as both **a public record** and **a training substrate** for large models: if machine learning systems ingest the open web, then **small, distributed distortions** are not only a human annoyance but a parable about **tainted learning** - data that looks authoritative because of the channel it arrives through.

The title compresses **wiki** and **slavery** into an ugly neologism on purpose: it names a dependency loop where **human attention is extracted** to repair what machines and scripts break open.

## Context

The project was developed during **Con.Divisione 12 Art Residency** in **2023**.

## Skills involved

The work sits at the intersection of **automation**, **platform politics**, and **real-time staging**: writing software that can run for the length of an exhibition, deciding what should never be shown, and keeping the conceptual frame legible so the piece reads as critique of infrastructure rather than random vandalism.
